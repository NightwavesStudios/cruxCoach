Movement Learning & Practice

Overview

In climbing, and many other things, their are 3 stages of learning:

  • Cognitive: You need to actively think about the skill to preform it and can find basic applications for the skill likely in the flash level or below.

  • Diversification: You will reconize more oppurtunities to preform the skill more easily likely in your normal climbing or below.

  • Autonomous: You will almost or truly automatically do it in applicable scenarios in redpoint and project attempts. The technique is now close to - or is - muscle memory.

One cannot learn a skill and immediately take it into their practice in their most challenging climbing attempts or even through on-level attempts. Your body needs time to go through the 3 stages (cognitive, diversification, and finally autonomous) over time and different stages of practice. These stages of practice are:

  • General Paterning (Cognitive): Low intensity applying the technique to the easiest of problems to get a feel for it. Your attention shouldn't be on the route your climbing but on the new technique your applying. This stage can take minutes (for easy concepts like silent-feet) or days (for more advanced concepts like turning and drop knees).

  • Basic Application (Diversification): Still low intensity, likely flash level, where you practice the skill and apply it to slightly harder problems.

  • Submaximal Application (Diversification): Medium intensity again at your flash or even on-level if comfortable. At this point you are trying to apply the technique to unique wall angles, hold types, and types of problems (ex: endurance, big moves).

Applying the Stages to Climbing Technique

Now that we more deeply understand the 3 stages of learning: cognitive, diversification, and autonomous, as well as the 4 stages of practice: general paterning, basic application, submaximal application, and performance application, we can apply them when learning a new technique. For the sake of this lesson, we are going to be learning how to foot swap.

Stage Name When to Move On Intensity
General Paterning When you can apply the technique to the most basic of climbs (V0s or V1s) Very Low (V0-V2)
Basic Application When you can apply the technique to climbs at your flash level (V2s or V3s) Low
Submaximal Application When you can apply the technique to climbs at your redpoint level (V4s or V5s) Medium
Performance Application No moving from here! High-Max

Step 1: Learn

Before we can even jump on a wall, we must understand the technique we are trying to apply to our climbing. This means searching up the technique (foot swapping in this case) and watching a video or reading a lesson. Only once we understand how to do it can we begin applying it to basic climbing.

Step 2: General Paterning

Now that we better understand the technique, we can apply it to very basic climbing. This climbing should be below flash level and probably some of the easiest climbs in your gym. For example, to learn foot swapping, I would go on a V0 or V1 and simply practice swapping my feet at the start holds. Only once I am very comfortable applying the technique to the most basic of climbing would I then move up.

Step 3: Basic Application

Now, I will start to find climbs at my flash level or (ideally) below for foot swapping, I will try to apply it to easy climbs such as V2s or V3s. The climb still shouldn't give you much trouble and your main focus should be correctly applying your technique.

Step 4: Submaximal Application

Congrats!

Notes & Tips:

  • Applying a technique at V2 can look very different than at V4, thus slow progression. While the basic pattern may be the same on the V2 & V4, the reconizing of when to apply it and how to apply it will be different.
